Improving Public Speaking Techniques and Skills

The lights are bright, the stage is hot ... imagine stepping to the podium in front of a group of strangers, colleagues, and peers and feeling nothing but confidence in your ability to communicate your message. In the professional world and beyond, public speaking skills are among the most sought after, yet many people feel held back by anxiety, poor articulation, or a general feeling of “I’m not a natural at this.” Fortunately, there exist numerous tools to sharpen your public speaking technique and become a poised, articulate, and confident presenter ready to handle even the most intimidating scenarios and unexpected questions.

Why is public speaking so frightening?

What is it about speaking in front of an audience that gives the majority of people serious qualms? This haunting stage fright - which prevents many of us from speaking at professional and social events - stems from a variety of reasons:

  • Biology: Being put on the spot often activates our body’s sympathetic nervous system (aka stress mode), giving us a racing heart, sweaty palms, and a shaky voice. A person’s baseline level of public speaking anxiety correlates with their genetic predisposition, as well as past traumatic experiences. 
  • Lack of experience: If speaking in front of a crowd is only done twice per year, you can imagine it will be more difficult than if you’re leading the meeting every week. Repeated positive experiences allow us to feel more comfortable and confident when delivering presentations. 
  • Fear of being judged: Presenting in front of a group of people — no matter how large — causes many speakers to feel concerned about not meeting expectations and being judged by their audience.
  • Imposter syndrome: Many people battle subconscious fears that they are not truly up for the task at hand. Public speaking can exacerbate imposter syndrome, making a speaker fearful that the stage will shine a spotlight on any incompetencies.
  • Anxiety about making a mistake: From stumbling over words to giving an incorrect statistic, it's normal to worry about the multitude of things that could go wrong during a presentation.
  • Fear of looking unprepared: Most presentations include time for questions and answers. This can be nerve-wracking as speakers don't know exactly what to expect from their audience and may fear being put on the spot.
  • Discomfort with your voice and body: Preparing to speak in front of an audience can feel physically uncomfortable. Many speakers worry about looking natural and properly managing body language such as hand gestures, posture, eye contact, and breathing.

America's Greatest Fear Needn't Be Yours

If you’ve ever watched TED Talks and thought, “are they just naturals at this?” you’re not alone. The truth is, many of the speakers you most admire have decades of speech training under their belts.

Our public speaking training guides you through the steps to present the best version of yourself. We discuss how to project relaxed enthusiasm, pleasant assertiveness, and grounded vitality. We'll cover topics ranging from body work (“What do I do with my hands?!” ”Should I walk around?”) to voice control (“What do I do when my voice starts to quiver?” “How can I project without yelling?”) to handling anxiety (“My mind goes blank when I get nervous!” “How can I stop my heart from racing?”) to impromptu speaking (“I’m fine when I know my content in advance, but I hate being put on the spot!”). Public speaking is a wonderful discipline that's surprisingly underserved, and once we work on some fundamental principles together, you'll find that you are able to see exciting results — even after your first lesson.

Creating Practical Skills for Speaking in Public

Fortunately, finding ways to improve your public speaking abilities doesn't have to be daunting. Becoming a powerful public speaker requires mastering a specific set of skills, which you can sharpen through time, effort, feedback, and plenty of practice. Developing these public speaking abilities will put you on the path to a successful speech:

  • Authenticity: An audience can sense when you're being yourself. Most importantly, speaking authentically will help you to feel calm and confident throughout your presentation. Instead of "faking it until you make it," speak honestly and use language that reflects who you are and what you know.
  • Confidence: Confidence is key to appearing credible, knowledgeable, and accurate. Being truly confident in front of a crowd is not easy — but it is achievable, and one of the most important practical skills for speaking in public.
  • Articulation: The best public speakers are easy to understand. Excellent articulation includes an entire set of skills, from enunciating clearly and speaking loudly to eliminating the use of filler words and using proper grammar.
  • Engagement: Strong public speakers can engage their audience and keep them interested, no matter the topic. Becoming a commanding presence might come more naturally to some individuals than others, but at its core, it is a learned skill that anybody can master. A skilled presentation style includes developing a varied vocal tone, engaging facial expressions, and using appropriate timing.
  • Interaction: It takes time and practice to learn how to properly interact with your audience — but it's one of the most important ways to communicate your message. Between asking good questions, requesting volunteers, and using strong eye contact, the best presentations are two-way dialogues.
  • Personability: Do you want to learn how to grow as a speaker? Connecting with your audience is crucial. Developing personable skills — such as the ability to tell engaging stories and adapt your presentation style to fit your audience — will take you far in your journey to becoming an outstanding public speaker.

How does private instruction differ from the public speaking courses at my office?

What about one-on-one or informal situations?

“Public speaking skills” are not limited to presentations in front of auditoriums and board rooms. The techniques can be applied to a one-on-one meeting with a client just as easily as for a big speech!
